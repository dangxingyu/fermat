<strategy>
Primary approach: Two-part proof — existence by strong induction, uniqueness by
  Euclid's Lemma (itself derived from the Division Lemma via Bézout's identity)
Why this approach: The claim splits naturally into existence and uniqueness.
  Existence is a clean strong induction: every composite has a smaller factor,
  so the induction hypothesis gives prime factorizations of the parts. Uniqueness
  is where the real content lives — you need to show that if a prime p divides a
  product ab, then p | a or p | b. This is the irreducibility-implies-primality
  step, and it flows from the Division Lemma through Bézout's identity. This
  mirrors *why* the theorem is true: the Division Lemma is what gives ℤ a
  Euclidean structure, and that structure is what forces unique factorization.
Alternatives considered: One could appeal to the well-ordering principle directly
  for existence (take the least divisor > 1, which must be prime, repeat) — this
  is essentially equivalent to strong induction but slightly less transparent for
  the uniqueness half. Alternatively, uniqueness can be proved by assuming two
  factorizations and canceling common primes, but this still requires Euclid's
  Lemma underneath. Neither alternative avoids the Bézout gap identified below.
</strategy>

<key_steps>
PART 1 — EXISTENCE

1. Base case: n = 2 is prime, so it is already a product of one prime (itself).
   Technique: direct verification using def:prime
   Depends on: def:prime

2. Inductive step (strong induction): Assume every integer 2 ≤ k < n has a
   prime factorization. If n is prime, done. If n is composite, then n = ab
   with 1 < a, b < n; by the induction hypothesis both a and b have prime
   factorizations, and concatenating them gives one for n.
   Technique: strong induction; definition of composite (negation of def:prime)
   Depends on: def:prime

PART 2 — UNIQUENESS

3. Establish Euclid's Lemma: if p is prime and p | ab, then p | a or p | b.
   Sub-proof: since p is prime, gcd(p, a) is 1 or p. If p | a, done. Otherwise
   gcd(p, a) = 1, so by Bézout there exist x, y with px + ay = 1; multiply
   both sides by b to get pxb + aby = b; since p | p and p | ab, p | b.
   Technique: Bézout's identity (needs lem:bezout), divisibility arithmetic
   Depends on: lem:division → lem:bezout (both missing)

4. Extend Euclid's Lemma to products: if p | p₁p₂⋯pₖ, then p = pᵢ for some i
   (since each pᵢ is prime, p | pᵢ forces p = pᵢ).
   Technique: induction on k using step 3
   Depends on: step 3, def:prime

5. Uniqueness argument: suppose n = p₁⋯pₛ = q₁⋯qₜ are two prime factorizations.
   p₁ divides the right-hand side, so by step 4, p₁ = qⱼ for some j. Cancel
   and repeat; the two lists must be identical up to reordering, so s = t.
   Technique: cancellation induction on the length of the factorization
   Depends on: step 4
</key_steps>
