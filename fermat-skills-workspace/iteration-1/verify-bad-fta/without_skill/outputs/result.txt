## Assessment: NEEDS_REVISION

The overall proof strategy is sound and the key ideas are correct, but there are several gaps and issues that require attention.

---

### Issues

**1. Existence: Induction type not specified precisely**

The inductive step implicitly uses *strong induction* (the hypothesis covers all $k$ with $2 \leq k < n$), but the proof opens with "We prove by induction on $n$" without specifying this is strong induction. This should be stated explicitly.

**2. Uniqueness: The induction base case is not handled**

The uniqueness argument says "cancel and apply induction," but it never verifies the base case for uniqueness separately, nor does it confirm that the resulting shorter product $p_2 \cdots p_r = q_1 \cdots \hat{q}_j \cdots q_s$ corresponds to a value strictly less than $n$ (though this is true, it should be stated). More critically, the argument must address the case where after cancellation one side is empty (i.e., $r = 1$ or $s = 1$), to conclude $r = s$.

**3. The key lemma is used without proof or citation**

The step "since $p_1$ divides the right side, $p_1$ must divide some $q_j$" relies on **Euclid's lemma**: if a prime $p$ divides a product $q_1 \cdots q_s$, then $p \mid q_j$ for some $j$. This is a nontrivial lemma (ultimately depending on Bézout's identity) and is the logical core of the uniqueness proof. Invoking it without proof or citation is a significant gap.

**4. Uniqueness induction is incomplete**

The uniqueness argument does not explicitly state what the inductive hypothesis is for uniqueness, nor does it close the argument (e.g., concluding $r = s$ and that the factorizations are identical up to reordering).

---

### Summary of Required Revisions

| Issue | Severity |
|---|---|
| "Induction" should be specified as strong induction | Minor |
| Euclid's lemma invoked without justification | **Major** |
| Base case for uniqueness not addressed | Moderate |
| Inductive conclusion for uniqueness not closed | Moderate |

---

The proof is **not wrong in its reasoning**, but as written it omits the hardest mathematical content (Euclid's lemma) and leaves the uniqueness argument insufficiently rigorous to stand on its own.
